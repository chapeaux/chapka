# Open Source and AI Contributions: A Landscape Guide

*Synthesized from policies and discussions across major open source communities, early 2026.*

AI-assisted coding tools are now part of the everyday developer workflow. Open source communities are actively establishing norms for how these tools interact with contribution processes, licensing frameworks, and community trust. This document synthesizes the current state of play from over a dozen major projects and communities, identifies common themes, and offers practical guidance for both policy-makers and individual contributors.

---

## General Sentiment Across Communities

Community responses to AI-assisted contributions cluster into three broad postures. No major project has adopted an outright ban, but the degree of welcome varies significantly.

### Permissive with Guardrails

Projects like the **Linux Kernel**, **Fedora**, the **Pulp Project**, and the **Avocado Framework** accept AI-assisted contributions as a natural evolution of tooling. Their policies focus on disclosure, accountability, and maintaining existing quality standards rather than restricting use.

- The Linux Kernel treats AI tools similarly to other code-generation tools (like Coccinelle), requiring an `Assisted-by:` tag and full compliance with the Developer Certificate of Origin (DCO).
- Fedora uses RFC 2119 language ("MAY", "MUST", "SHOULD") to set clear expectations: contributors MAY use AI, MUST take responsibility, and MUST disclose when significant portions are AI-generated. Routine grammar and spelling corrections are exempt.
- Pulp requires `Assisted By: <Tool>` or `Generated by: <Tool>` labels in commit messages and verification of copyright compliance for any AI-generated material.
- Avocado Framework requires explicit attribution in both commit messages and PR descriptions, including an estimate of the percentage of AI-generated content. Contributors must enable code similarity matching features in their AI tools to guard against training data leakage.

**Zulip** occupies a pragmatic middle ground within this posture. Its policy centers on maintainer time as a scarce resource: "changes you ask us to review represent your *best* work." AI tools are welcome for understanding the codebase and writing code, but PRs that appear to be untested AI output are closed without review. Zulip also explicitly addresses AI in communication — AI-generated messages are prohibited in the development community, and PR descriptions must focus on the "why" rather than regurgitating what an LLM sees in the diff.

### Cautious and Conditional

**Node.js**, **Django**, and **OpenSSL** are actively debating policies, with discussions revealing both enthusiasm and deep concern.

- Node.js (PR #62105) proposes that contributors must "own every line" regardless of how it was generated, verify accuracy against actual source code, and avoid posting entirely AI-generated communications. The policy underwent substantial revision during TSC review and was awaiting a formal vote as of early 2026.
- Django's forum discussion, modeled after the attrs approach, surfaced productive disagreement about whether `Assisted-by:` commit trailers or `Co-authored-by:` tags are more appropriate, and whether existing contribution guidelines already suffice.
- OpenSSL's discussion centers on CLA compliance gaps: AI models train on code from unknown authors who haven't signed CLAs, creating legal uncertainty that existing agreements don't address.

### Restrictive

**attrs** represents the most restrictive published policy. Pull requests listing LLM products as co-authors are rejected immediately. Unsupervised agentic tools (like OpenClaw) are explicitly prohibited. Accounts exhibiting automated mass submissions face permanent bans. LLM-generated review comments are not permitted.

The attrs policy reflects frustration with "vibe coding loops" — where contributors repeatedly feed LLM outputs back for iteration without meaningful human judgment — rather than opposition to AI tooling in principle.

### Common Ground

Despite differing postures, all communities agree on core principles:

- **Humans are accountable.** No project accepts "an LLM wrote it" as a defense for problematic code.
- **AI is not an author.** No policy grants authorship, co-authorship, or contributor credit to an AI system.
- **Existing standards apply.** AI-assisted contributions must meet the same quality, licensing, and review standards as any other contribution.

## Categories of Consideration

Six recurring themes appear across policies and discussions. Any community drafting an AI contribution policy should consider each.

### 1. Verification and Quality

Every policy examined requires contributors to understand and be able to defend everything they submit. The Linux Kernel explicitly states that maintainers can reject patches without detailed review if submitters cannot demonstrate comprehension of generated code. Node.js emphasizes that contributors should "familiarize yourself with the relevant subsystem" and verify LLM claims against actual source code. Zulip warns contributors not to trust claims LLMs make about how the codebase works — "LLMs are often wrong, even about details that are clearly answered in the Zulip documentation."

Maintainers retain full discretion. The Kernel policy lists options ranging from normal acceptance to outright rejection to demands for extra testing. Fedora notes that AI tools "MUST NOT" serve as the sole or final arbiter of substantive judgments on contributions. Zulip will close PRs without review if they appear to be untested AI output. Avocado requires that review of AI-generated code be "as rigorous, if not more so, than the review of human-written code."

### 2. Attribution and Disclosure

The `Assisted-by:` commit message trailer has emerged as a de facto standard across communities. The Linux Kernel specifies a format including tool name, model version, and any specialized analysis tools:

```
Assisted-by: Claude:claude-3-opus coccinelle sparse
```

Fedora recommends similar trailers:

```
Assisted-by: ChatGPTv5
Assisted-by: generic LLM chatbot
```

Pulp distinguishes between `Assisted By:` (human-directed use) and `Generated by:` (primarily automated output).

Avocado goes further, requesting that contributors estimate the percentage of AI-generated content alongside the `Assisted-by:` tag. It also distinguishes between substantive AI use (requiring explicit attribution in both commit and PR) and brainstorming/refactoring use (where a general PR description disclosure suffices).

Most policies agree that AI tools should not receive `Co-authored-by:` credit. Django's discussion highlighted that listing AI as a co-author functions as advertisement rather than attribution. Several communities suggest that basic development tools (compilers, linters, formatters) need not be listed — the threshold is tools that generate meaningful content.

Fedora provides a useful calibration: routine use of assistive tools for correcting grammar, spelling, or clarifying language does not require disclosure. Disclosure is required when the significant part of the contribution is taken from a tool without changes.

### 3. Licensing and Copyright

This is the most legally complex area and the one with least consensus.

- **DCO/CLA compliance**: The Linux Kernel requires all contributions to be GPL-2.0-only compatible. AI agents MUST NOT add `Signed-off-by` tags — only humans can certify DCO compliance. Pulp requires contributors to verify they have permission for any copyrighted materials in AI output.
- **Training data leakage**: Avocado explicitly addresses this risk, requiring contributors to enable code similarity matching or blocking features in their AI tools when available. If leakage from proprietary or incompatibly licensed training data is identified, the code must either comply with the source license (and be compatible with the project's license) or be rewritten before submission.
- **Copyright uncertainty**: OpenSSL's discussion identified that AI models train on code with unknown provenance, creating situations where CLA compliance is uncertain. The attrs policy notes that without a formal CLA, AI-generated content creates unpredictable legal status.
- **Mixed human-AI works**: Django's discussion referenced the US Copyright Office's January 2025 report, which permits copyright protection for mixed human-AI works when human contributions are identified — countering claims that any AI involvement voids copyright.

### 4. Contributor Accountability

Every policy places full legal and technical responsibility on the human submitter. Key patterns:

- Contributors must certify they are the legal author or hold explicit authorization (attrs)
- Contributors must understand their entire submission and be prepared to respond to review comments (Linux Kernel)
- The final accountability for accepting a contribution always rests with the human who authorizes the action (Fedora)
- Node.js debated changing "expects" to "requires" in its policy language to strengthen accountability signals

### 5. Spam and Abuse Prevention

The CHAOSS WG AI Alignment working group has documented patterns of problematic AI use in open source:

- Mass pull requests with minimal value
- Automated bots posting unhelpful comments
- Code violating project practices or using outdated APIs
- Karma-farming comment spam

The attrs policy addresses this directly with permanent bans for accounts exhibiting automated mass submissions. Fedora carves out an exception for "large scale initiatives" that may significantly change project operations, requiring separate discussion with the Fedora Council. Zulip addresses a subtler form of spam: verbose AI-generated communication that wastes reviewer time. Its policy demands succinctness and prohibits AI-generated messages in the development community entirely, preferring "clear and concise communication about points that actually require discussion."

### 6. Enforceability

This is the elephant in the room. OpenSSL's discussion explicitly acknowledged that distinguishing AI-generated from human-written code is practically impossible. This makes blanket restrictions difficult to enforce.

Communities are addressing this through:

- **Trust-based models**: Relying on contributor honesty with social consequences for dishonesty
- **Tooling**: CHAOSS offers an AI detection GitHub Action and PR trust-scoring tools, though no detection method is fully reliable
- **Violation reporting**: Fedora directs concerns about policy violations to Pagure private tickets to the Fedora Council
- **Review rigor**: Several projects note that AI-assisted PRs often require heavier review, which serves as a natural quality filter

## Suggested AI Skills and Agents for Projects

Communities looking to integrate AI tooling productively should consider these categories of tools and practices.

### Project Context Files

Files like `CLAUDE.md`, `AGENTS.md`, or `.cursorrules` provide AI coding agents with project-specific context — coding conventions, architecture decisions, testing expectations, and contribution requirements. These files improve the quality of AI-generated output by grounding it in the project's actual practices rather than generic patterns.

### Code Review Assistants

AI-powered first-pass review can catch style violations, common bugs, and deviations from project patterns before human reviewers invest time. These work best as supplements to, not replacements for, human review. Fedora's policy explicitly permits automated tooling for objective technical validation.

### Test Generation Agents

Agents that generate test cases from existing code, specifications, or bug reports can expand test coverage without consuming contributor time on boilerplate. The generated tests should be reviewed and understood by contributors before submission.

### Documentation Agents

Keeping documentation in sync with code changes is a persistent challenge. AI agents can draft documentation updates when APIs, configurations, or behaviors change, with human review for accuracy and tone.

### Commit Message and Changelog Agents

Structured, consistent commit messages improve project history. AI agents can draft messages with proper attribution tags (`Assisted-by:`), conventional commit formatting, and meaningful summaries.

### CI/CD Integration

Automated checks for:
- PR template completeness, including AI disclosure fields
- Presence of `Assisted-by:` tags when AI use is declared
- Contribution size and pattern anomalies that may indicate automated mass submissions

### Detection and Scoring Tools

- **CHAOSS AI detection action**: Identifies potential machine-generated contributions
- **PR trust-scoring GitHub Actions**: Provide quality signals based on contribution patterns, contributor history, and content analysis

## Getting Started: Opening AI Contribution Discussion

Starting the conversation about AI in your community does not require arriving with a finished policy. Here are approaches that have worked.

### 1. Reference the Landscape

Present the spectrum of existing policies — from the Kernel's pragmatic approach to attrs' restrictive stance — and ask your community where they see themselves. The CHAOSS WG AI Alignment moderation resources provide a curated list of 20+ project policies, foundation guidance, and discussion links that serve as an excellent starting point.

### 2. Survey Current Usage

Many contributors are already using AI tools. An anonymous, non-judgmental survey of current practices gives the community real data to work from rather than debating hypotheticals. Questions might include which tools people use, for what tasks, and what concerns they have.

### 3. Start with a Lightweight RFC

Draft a short proposal referencing 2-3 existing policies as templates. Fedora's policy is a strong model for permissive-with-guardrails communities; attrs works for projects wanting stricter boundaries. Keep the initial proposal focused on disclosure and accountability — these are the areas with broadest consensus.

### 4. Frame Around Quality, Not Permission

The most productive discussions focus on maintaining contribution quality and reviewer bandwidth rather than debating whether AI should be "allowed." Communities that frame the discussion as "how do we maintain our standards as tools evolve" tend to reach consensus faster than those debating "should we permit AI."

## Getting Started: Submitting the First AI-Generated Pull Request

### Before You Start

1. **Check for an existing policy.** Search the project's CONTRIBUTING.md, governance docs, or repository for AI-related guidance. Follow it.
2. **If no policy exists**, be transparent and let your contribution start the conversation.

### Choosing the Right First Contribution

Start with something low-risk where AI assistance adds clear value:
- Documentation improvements or corrections
- Test case additions for uncovered code paths
- Small, well-defined bug fixes with clear reproduction steps
- Boilerplate reduction or repetitive code updates

Avoid AI-assisted contributions to security-sensitive code, complex architectural changes, or areas you don't already understand.

### The Submission

1. **Disclose AI usage in the PR description.** Briefly explain what tool you used and how. Mention whether the AI drafted the change, helped with specific portions, or served as a review aid.
2. **Add an `Assisted-by:` tag** in your commit message:
   ```
   Assisted-by: Claude:claude-3-opus
   ```
3. **Split AI output into coherent commits.** Even if an LLM generates changes in one go, break them into logical commits that tell a clear story (as Zulip's guidelines emphasize).
4. **Write your own PR description.** Focus on *why* you're making the change, not *what* changed — reviewers can see the diff. Don't let an LLM regurgitate what's already visible in the code.
5. **Demonstrate understanding.** In your PR description and review responses, show that you understand every line of the change, why it works, and what alternatives you considered.
6. **Be prepared to defend the code.** Respond to review comments with your own understanding, not by re-prompting an AI.

### If Things Go Sideways

Some reviewers may have strong feelings about AI-assisted contributions. Respond respectfully, be willing to withdraw or rework the PR, and treat pushback as data for the community's evolving norms rather than a personal rejection.

## Getting Started: Submitting Regular AI-Assisted Contributions

Once you've established a track record, these practices help make AI-assisted contributions sustainable and valuable.

### Establish Your Workflow

- **AI drafts, human reviews.** Use AI to generate initial code, then read, test, modify, and understand it before submission. The goal is efficiency, not abdication.
- **Use project context files.** If the project has a `CLAUDE.md`, `AGENTS.md`, or similar file, use it. If it doesn't, consider contributing one based on your experience with what the AI needs to know about the project.
- **Distinguish assistance levels.** Be honest about whether AI assisted a small portion of your work or generated the bulk of it. Different disclosure levels (Fedora's `Assisted-by` vs. Pulp's `Generated by`) help reviewers calibrate their scrutiny.

### Maintain Consistency

- Use the same attribution format across contributions
- Keep your AI tool configurations project-aware so output quality improves over time
- Track which types of contributions work well with AI assistance and which don't

### Contribute to Policy Evolution

Your experience as a regular AI-assisted contributor is valuable data for the community:
- Share what works and what doesn't in retrospectives or community discussions
- Help refine the project's AI policy based on practical experience
- Mentor other contributors on effective AI-assisted workflows

### Know the Boundaries

- AI-generated review comments are unwelcome in most communities — do your own reviews
- AI-generated messages in community channels are prohibited by several projects (Zulip, Node.js) — use your own words
- Don't ask LLMs to generate code comments; as Zulip notes, they typically produce unnecessary text explaining what's already clear from the code
- When referencing information, link to primary sources (source code, docs, standards) rather than quoting LLM answers
- Large-scale automated contributions (mass refactors, bulk PRs) typically require separate discussion with maintainers
- Some subsystems or contribution types may have stricter standards — respect them
